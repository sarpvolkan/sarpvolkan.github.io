## General Profile

I am a researcher in **space weather** with a background in systems engineering, developing computational methods to understand the ionosphere as a complex dynamical system. My research combines signal processing, complexity science, and solar-terrestrial physics to detect, characterize, and interpret patterns in large geophysical datasets. A central goal of my work is to develop data-driven methods that remain physically meaningful and explainable, bridging modern computational techniques with fundamental scientific understanding.

A recurring theme throughout my research is the adaptation of computational methodologies across disciplines. Rather than developing entirely new algorithms, I seek methods that have proven successful in other scientific domains and repurpose them. This approach has led me to adapt a particle-tracking framework originally developed for biomedical imaging to analyze fine-scale solar structures, and to apply nonlinear forecasting techniques from quantitative ecology to solar cycle prediction. More broadly, I view **interdisciplinary methodological transfer** as a powerful driver of scientific innovation.

My current research focuses on the coupled ionosphere–thermosphere system using GNSS (Global Navigation Satellite System)-derived Total Electron Content (TEC), one of the primary observational proxies for ionospheric electron density. I combine **multiscale signal processing, spatiotemporal analysis, and physics-based numerical modeling** to investigate how energy and disturbances propagate through the upper atmosphere. Recently, I developed a framework to isolate scale-dependent ionospheric responses to the April 2024 total solar eclipse using an event-centered reference frame aligned with the eclipse shadow cone. I am now extending this work through controlled simulation experiments with the Global Ionosphere-Thermosphere Model (GITM) to investigate the physical mechanisms responsible for the observed longitudinal asymmetries.

Looking ahead, I am interested in advancing **data-driven scientific discovery** in geospace science by integrating concepts from information theory, nonlinear time-series analysis, and artificial intelligence with established physical understanding. I believe future advances in space weather research will increasingly rely on computational methods that are both scientifically interpretable and operationally useful. My **long-term research vision** includes developing information-theoretic descriptions of ionospheric disturbances for next-generation space weather forecasting and exploiting ionosphere–thermosphere coupling to infer the sparsely observed thermospheric state from abundant ionospheric measurements, ultimately supporting more accurate orbital drag prediction and improved space situational awareness.

Beyond advancing fundamental understanding of geospace dynamics, I hope to contribute to the development of Türkiye's capabilities in ionospheric science, upper-atmosphere monitoring, and next-generation space weather forecasting while pursuing collaborations that extend these methodologies to other complex geophysical systems.
